The 24-Hour Follow-Up

By Yan · 4 min read

Picture the graveyard: every business card you've ever collected, every "great to meet you!" that led nowhere, every conference connection who evaporated by the following Tuesday. Acres of dead handshakes, as far as the eye can see.

None of those relationships died of natural causes. They died of no follow-up.

Keith Ferrazzi is emphatic about this in Never Eat Alone: following up isn't a nice-to-have after meeting someone — it's the entire difference between a contact and a relationship. His deadline is aggressive: within 12 to 24 hours, while you're still a face and a conversation rather than a name that rings a distant bell.

Why the window is so short

Memory decay is brutal and democratic. Tomorrow, the person you met is 40% gone from your mind — and you're 40% gone from theirs. A week later you're a lanyard. The follow-up note isn't politeness; it's a save button. You're writing yourself into their long-term memory before the autosave fails.

The anatomy of a follow-up that lands

  • Reference the actual conversation. "Loved your take on why every CRM eventually becomes a junk drawer" beats "great meeting you!" — because it proves the conversation happened and you were present for it.
  • Deliver something. The article you mentioned, the intro you offered, the name of that book. A follow-up that ships value on day one sets the tone for the entire relationship.
  • Plant the next touch, gently. "If you're ever near the office, tacos on me" is plenty. You're not closing a deal; you're leaving a door open.

The part nobody does: follow-up has a sequel

Here's where even the diligent fall down. They send the day-after note, feel virtuous (correctly), and then... nothing. Forever. The first follow-up saves the file; without a second touch a few weeks later — a ping, a link, a hello — the file just corrupts more slowly.

The habit that separates the people everyone remembers from the people nobody can quite place isn't charisma. It's that they follow up once within a day, again within a month, and quietly never stop. It's not magic. It's calendar discipline wearing magic's outfit.

Full disclosure: I built Call Your Mom because my own follow-up game was inconsistently consistent — two great weeks, then radio silence until someone's birthday shamed me. It watches for the right moment — a birthday, a promise coming due, a relationship going quiet — and hands you the nudge with the message already drafted. The warmth is still on you. The remembering is on us.
