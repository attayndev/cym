Tiny Hooks Do the Heavy Lifting

By Yan · 4 min read

Nobody remembers your quarterly newsletter. Everybody remembers that you called on their birthday.

This is one of the stranger asymmetries in human relationships: the gestures that matter most are almost embarrassingly small. A birthday message. A "good luck today." A follow-up on a promise everyone else forgot they made. Keith Ferrazzi builds a whole practice around this in Never Eat Alone — he treats birthdays and personal milestones not as fluff, but as scheduled opportunities to prove someone occupies real estate in your mind.

Why tiny hooks outperform grand gestures

A hook — a birthday, a due date, a thing they said they were dreading — carries built-in proof that you were paying attention. Anyone can send a message. The message that lands on the right day, referencing the right thing, is unfakeable. It says: I logged this, I carried it, and I showed up on schedule. That's the entire emotional payload, delivered in one sentence.

Grand gestures, meanwhile, are so rare and effortful they mostly exist in our intentions. The dinner we'll organize someday. The long letter we'll write. Tiny hooks actually ship.

The three hooks worth tracking

  • Birthdays. The classic for a reason. Skip the wall post everyone else writes; send the private message, or — power move from a bygone era — make the phone call. Ninety seconds, remembered for a year.
  • Promises. "I'll send you that intro." "Let me dig up that doc." Every promise you make is a hook you've planted in someone else's expectations. Deliver them on time and you become that rarest of creatures: a person whose word executes.
  • Their big moments. The launch, the surgery, the kid's first day of school, the review they were dreading. A message the morning of — "thinking of you today, go get it" — is intimacy on a schedule. It costs nothing. It's worth everything.

The catch: your brain is a sieve

Here's the honest problem. You care about these people, and you will still forget the birthday, the promise, and the surgery — not from indifference, but because you have a job and a life and a phone that buzzes 400 times a day. Caring was never the bottleneck. Remembering is.

So build yourself a memory. Write the hook down the moment it's spoken, put the date somewhere that will tap your shoulder, and let the system be reliable so you can focus on being warm. The people on the receiving end will just think you have a supernatural heart. No one needs to know it had backup.

Full disclosure: I built Call Your Mom because my own follow-up game was inconsistently consistent — two great weeks, then radio silence until someone's birthday shamed me. It watches for the right moment — a birthday, a promise coming due, a relationship going quiet — and hands you the nudge with the message already drafted. The warmth is still on you. The remembering is on us.
