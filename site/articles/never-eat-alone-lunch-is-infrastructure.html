Never Eat Alone: Lunch Is Infrastructure

By Yan · 4 min read

The title idea of Keith Ferrazzi's Never Eat Alone is so simple it feels like a trick: you're going to eat anyway — several times a day, if things are going well — so stop doing it over your keyboard and start doing it with humans.

A meal is the only meeting nobody has to justify. There's no agenda, no deck, no action items. Just food and the ancient social technology of sitting across from someone. It's the lowest-stakes, highest-warmth container for a relationship that exists — and most of us spend it with a sandwich and eleven browser tabs.

The calendar audit

This week you have roughly five workday lunches and a couple of coffee-shaped gaps. That's seven relationship slots you're currently donating to your desk. You don't need all seven — one is plenty. One lunch a week is 50 warm touchpoints a year, which is more than most people manage in a decade of "we should totally catch up."

Making it happen without making it weird

  • Invite specifically. "Want to grab lunch Thursday? There's a taco place near you I've been meaning to try" converts about five times better than "we should get lunch sometime," which is not an invitation — it's a pleasantry wearing an invitation's clothes.
  • Stack the deck. Ferrazzi's power move: invite two people who'd benefit from knowing each other. You eat one lunch; three relationships get warmer; you're now the person who makes useful things happen over guacamole.
  • Keep it cheap and short. This is not a state dinner. Forty-five minutes and a burrito outperforms two hours of white tablecloths, because the forty-five minute version actually happens more than once a year.

For my fellow introverts

I hear you: sometimes lunch alone is the only thing holding the day together. Keep those. The point isn't to monetize every meal like some kind of networking dystopia — it's that the default of always eating alone quietly starves relationships that would flourish on the most renewable resource you have.

You have to eat anyway. Occasionally, add a witness.
