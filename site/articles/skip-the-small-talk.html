Skip the Small Talk

By Yan · 4 min read

Small talk is a mutual agreement to say nothing, politely, until one of you can escape to the snack table. Nobody has ever left a conversation about traffic thinking "I need that person in my life."

One of the more counterintuitive arguments in Keith Ferrazzi's Never Eat Alone is that the safe, professional, weather-adjacent script is exactly backwards. The people we remember are the ones who were briefly, appropriately real with us. Ferrazzi argues that what actually bonds people — even in business — are the things we're all privately preoccupied with: our health, our families, our money worries, our ambitions. The stuff small talk is specifically designed to avoid.

Why realness works

When you offer something genuine — "honestly, this year's been a rebuild" — you hand the other person permission to be a person too. That trade of tiny vulnerabilities is the actual mechanism of connection. It's also efficient: one honest exchange builds more relationship than a year of "how about this weather," which builds approximately none.

The fear is that realness looks unprofessional. The evidence says otherwise — we trust people we know, and we can't know someone who only ever performs.

Going deeper without going full therapy session

There's obviously a line. "My deepest fear is dying unremarkable" is a lot for the appetizer course. Some calibrated openers:

  • Ask questions with actual stakes. "What are you excited about right now?" or "what's the hard part of your job lately?" — both invite a real answer while leaving the escape hatch of a shallow one.
  • Go first, at 30% depth. Share something true but survivable — a real struggle from your project, a genuine worry, a thing you changed your mind about. They'll usually meet you there.
  • Remember what they trusted you with. This is the part everyone skips. If someone mentions their mother's health and you ask about her next time — that's the relationship. Made of exactly that.

The compounding effect

Depth remembered is depth multiplied. The conversation only counts if it survives your memory — write down what mattered before your brain overwrites it with whatever Slack does to you at 9am tomorrow. The person who asks about the marathon, the surgery, the terrifying new job? That person is unforgettable, and it costs them thirty seconds of note-taking a day.

Full disclosure: I built Call Your Mom because my own follow-up game was inconsistently consistent — two great weeks, then radio silence until someone's birthday shamed me. It watches for the right moment — a birthday, a promise coming due, a relationship going quiet — and hands you the nudge with the message already drafted. The warmth is still on you. The remembering is on us.
