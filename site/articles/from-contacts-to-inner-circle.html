From 2,000 Contacts to an Inner Circle

By Yan · 5 min read

Open your phone contacts and scroll. Somewhere between "Aaron (conference?)" and "Zoe Plumber NOT GOOD," a truth sets in: this isn't a network. It's an archaeology site.

Two thousand contacts, and maybe thirty people who'd take your call at midnight. Both numbers are fine! The dysfunction is treating the whole pile like one undifferentiated blob called "my network" — which usually means the inner thirty get neglected while the outer thousands get nothing anyway.

A central practice in Keith Ferrazzi's Never Eat Alone is refusing to treat all relationships identically. He sorts relationships into tiers and gives each tier its own contact rhythm — intimates constantly, the active middle regularly, the wider circle at least annually. The specific labels matter less than the discipline: different circles, different cadences.

The three circles, roughly

  • The inner circle (10–30 people). Family, close friends, the mentors and allies who shaped you. These relationships want monthly contact minimum, and actual phone calls. This is also where your mother lives, and she has opinions about your call frequency. She is right.
  • The active circle (50–150). Current colleagues, clients, collaborators, good friends one ring out. Quarterly-ish rhythm — a ping, a lunch, a real check-in. This circle is where careers quietly get made.
  • The wide circle (everyone else worth keeping). Once or twice a year: the birthday message, the congrats, the "thought of you." Just enough current to keep the wire from corroding — so that when either of you reaches out, it's a reunion, not a resurrection.

Triage without guilt

And then there's the fourth group nobody discusses: contacts that aren't relationships at all. The plumber from the old apartment. The car dealership that texts on your birthday with more consistency than some relatives (a low bar we'll discuss another time). Archive them cheerfully. Pruning dead weight isn't coldness — it's what makes the real circles visible.

Rhythm beats intensity

The compounding secret of the circle system: it converts "networking" from a heroic occasional effort into a small daily one. A couple of touches a day, aimed at the right circle at the right interval, keeps hundreds of relationships genuinely warm. No hustle, no schmoozing, no name tags. Just people, remembered on purpose, at the rhythm they deserve.

Full disclosure: I built Call Your Mom because my own follow-up game was inconsistently consistent — two great weeks, then radio silence until someone's birthday shamed me. It watches for the right moment — a birthday, a promise coming due, a relationship going quiet — and hands you the nudge with the message already drafted. The warmth is still on you. The remembering is on us.
