Build Your Network Before You Need It

By Yan · 4 min read

There's a specific flavor of LinkedIn message that everyone can smell from a mile away. It usually starts with "Hey! Long time!" and ends with a favor. The sender hasn't said a word to you in six years, and now — coincidentally, the same week their company did layoffs — they "would love to catch up."

We've all received that message. If we're being honest, most of us have sent that message. I know I have. It didn't work.

Keith Ferrazzi's Never Eat Alone is built around a blunt observation: the moment you need a network is the worst possible moment to start building one. Relationships are like retirement accounts — the compounding only works if you fund them early, consistently, and while you don't need the money.

Desperation has a smell

When you reach out only in emergencies, every touchpoint carries an invoice. People feel it instantly. But when you've been checking in for years — congratulating, introducing, occasionally just saying "saw this and thought of you" — asking for help isn't a transaction. It's a Tuesday.

Ferrazzi's point isn't cynical. It's the opposite: start relationships when you have nothing to gain, because that's the only time people can be sure you're not gaming them.

The unglamorous mechanics

  • Reach out when nothing is wrong. The highest-value message you can send is one with no ask attached. It's also, weirdly, the rarest.
  • Keep a loose schedule. Not a spreadsheet with conditional formatting (I've been there; it's a cry for help). Just a rhythm — some people quarterly, some people monthly, your mom weekly. She counts. She counts double.
  • Bank goodwill in good times. When your career is going well, you have surplus: intros, advice, referrals, credibility. Spend it on other people. It comes back with interest, at the exact moment interest rates matter.

Start embarrassingly small

You don't need to "network" this week. You need to text one person you genuinely like and have accidentally ignored since last spring. That's the entire assignment. Tomorrow, another one.

Future-you — the one holding the layoff email or the half-finished pitch deck — will owe present-you a very nice dinner.

Full disclosure: I built Call Your Mom because my own follow-up game was inconsistently consistent — two great weeks, then radio silence until someone's birthday shamed me. It watches for the right moment — a birthday, a promise coming due, a relationship going quiet — and hands you the nudge with the message already drafted. The warmth is still on you. The remembering is on us.
