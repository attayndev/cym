The Art of the Ping

By Yan · 5 min read

Here's an uncomfortable exercise. Think of someone who mattered to you professionally three years ago — a mentor, a great colleague, a client you genuinely liked. Now answer honestly: if you texted them today, would you need an opening paragraph of apology?

That apology tax is what silence costs. And it compounds.

In Never Eat Alone, Keith Ferrazzi calls the antidote "pinging" — short, frequent, almost trivially light touches that keep a relationship warm between the big moments. Not lunches. Not calls. Twenty to ninety seconds of genuine contact: a congratulations, a link, a memory, a terrible pun if the relationship can structurally support it.

Why tiny beats epic

Most of us operate on the Epic Catch-Up model: let a relationship go quiet for two years, then attempt to revive it with a ninety-minute dinner and a bottle of wine. Lovely when it happens. It almost never happens — because scheduling it is a three-week email thread and both of you are tired.

Pings invert the model. Instead of one heroic touchpoint a year, you make twelve tiny ones. The relationship never drops below room temperature, so no single message has to carry the weight of resurrection. You stop paying the apology tax entirely.

A ping menu, ranked by effort

  • The occasion ping (10 seconds): birthday, work anniversary, funding announcement. Low effort, but timing does all the work.
  • The thought-of-you ping (30 seconds): an article, a meme, a restaurant. The message underneath: you exist in my head when you're not in the room. That's the whole gift.
  • The callback ping (60 seconds): "How did the board meeting go?" Referencing something they told you is memory as a love language — the professional version.
  • The no-reason ping (90 seconds): "No agenda — you crossed my mind. How's life?" The rarest and most powerful message in professional life. Sends the strongest signal precisely because it can't be faked into a sales funnel.

The line between pinging and pestering

One rule keeps you safe: a ping is for them, not from your pipeline. If your check-in has a hidden agenda wearing a trench coat, people spot it. Ping because the relationship deserves maintenance — the asks, when they come someday, will take care of themselves.

Two pings a day is fewer than 15 minutes a week. That's a warm network for less time than you spend picking something to watch.
